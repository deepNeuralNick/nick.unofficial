Training a Neural Network to Recognize My Coffee Mug

October 15, 2025 8 min read Computer Vision PyTorch Fun Project

Let's be honest ‚Äì in the world of AI research, we often tackle grand challenges like curing diseases or achieving AGI. But sometimes, the most personally important problem is much simpler: Can I build a neural network that recognizes my favorite coffee mug?

The Problem Statement

My kitchen has approximately 47 different mugs (don't ask how this happened), but there's only ONE that's perfect for my morning coffee. It's the right size, the perfect weight, and it has this little chip on the handle that somehow makes it even better. The problem? Other people in my household keep using it.

Solution: Train a computer vision model to detect when MY mug is being used by someone else, so I can reclaim it immediately. This is clearly the most important application of deep learning ever conceived.

The Dataset Challenge

First challenge: collecting data. I spent a weekend taking photos of my mug from every conceivable angle, in different lighting conditions, with different backgrounds. My family thinks I've lost my mind, but they don't understand the importance of good training data.

  • Positive samples: 1,247 images of THE mug
  • Negative samples: 3,891 images of inferior mugs
  • Augmentations: Rotations, color jitter, because coffee mugs don't always sit perfectly

Model Architecture

I went with a fine-tuned ResNet-50 because:

  1. It's proven to work well on object recognition tasks
  2. The pre-trained weights already understand basic shapes and textures
  3. I'm lazy and didn't want to train from scratch
import torch.nn as nn
from torchvision import models

class CoffeeMugClassifier(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, num_classes=2):
        super().__init__()
        self.backbone = models.resnet50(pretrained=True)
        self.backbone.fc = nn.Linear(2048, num_classes)
    
    def forward(self, x):
        return self.backbone(x)

The Results

After 15 epochs of training (and way too much coffee), the results were surprisingly good:

  • Training Accuracy: 98.7%
  • Validation Accuracy: 94.2%
  • False Positive Rate: 2.1% (acceptable mug confusion)

Real-World Testing

The moment of truth came when I deployed this to a Raspberry Pi with a camera pointed at the kitchen counter. The first day, it correctly identified 23 out of 24 mug appearances. The one false negative? My mug was hiding behind a cereal box.

More importantly, it successfully detected when my brother was using MY mug and sent me a Slack notification. Justice was served (along with perfectly brewed coffee in the correct vessel).

Lessons Learned

This silly project actually taught me several valuable lessons:

  1. Data quality matters more than quantity: 50 really good photos beat 500 mediocre ones
  2. Domain-specific problems are easier than you think: When you control the environment, even simple models work well
  3. Edge deployment is the real challenge: Getting 94% accuracy is easy; getting it to run on a Pi without melting is harder
  4. Sometimes the best projects solve personal problems: I now have uninterrupted access to my perfect coffee mug

What's Next?

Obviously, this is just the beginning. I'm now working on a companion model that can detect the optimal coffee-to-milk ratio based on visual analysis of the liquid color. Because if you're going to over-engineer your morning routine, you might as well go all the way.

The code for this project is available on my GitHub (link coming soon), and yes, I'm aware this is probably the most ridiculous use of computer vision ever documented. But my coffee tastes better knowing it's in the right mug, and isn't that what technology is really for?
